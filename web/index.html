How to research a social issue

Researching a social issue means more than collecting links. A useful article should explain the problem, identify who is affected, map the institutions involved, check available data, and name practical routes for action. The goal is not only to know more, but to make wiser decisions with people who are directly affected.

Basic research workflow

  1. Define the question. Write the issue as a question that can be answered or improved.
  2. Map affected people. Identify who experiences the issue and avoid treating them as only data points.
  3. Collect source types. Use official pages, service providers, academic work, journalism, and field conversations.
  4. Check reliability. Separate observed facts, legal rules, service claims, opinions, and unverified notes.
  5. Map the system. List institutions, eligibility rules, bottlenecks, funding routes, and responsibilities.
  6. Find practical pathways. Turn research into next steps: where to go, who to call, what to prepare, and what to avoid.
  7. Keep revision notes. Social information changes, so each article needs dates, sources, and update habits.

Every future issue page should follow this pattern: overview, affected groups, system map, support pathways, open questions, resource directory, and references.

Homelessness in Berlin

This is the first social issue article in the hub. It gathers public information about homelessness-related support in Berlin and turns it into an easy-to-scan orientation page. The aim is not to replace service providers, but to make the first route through the information easier to understand.

The article separates urgent help, official support routes, seasonal services, and ways to help responsibly. Linked sources should be checked before travel or outreach, because opening times, phone lines, seasonal offers, and eligibility details can change.

Urgent help

In an immediate emergency, use the regular emergency numbers: 112 for medical emergencies or fire, and 110 for police emergencies. For cold-weather risk, Berlin Kältehilfe provides seasonal information about overnight places, night cafes, meeting points, soup kitchens, and cold aid contacts.

Situation First action Notes
Immediate danger Call 112 or 110 Use 112 for medical/fire emergencies and 110 for police emergencies.
Cold weather exposure Check Berlin Kältehilfe Services are seasonal. Ask for consent before calling an outreach bus when possible.
Threatened housing loss Contact Soziale Wohnhilfe District social offices are the official first route for threatened or existing homelessness.

Support routes

A practical route usually begins with safety, then a sleeping place, food and hygiene, health support, documents, and longer-term housing advice. Berlin district offices and specialist counselling services are important because some support depends on legal status, income, district responsibility, or individual circumstances.

People facing homelessness and severe social difficulties may be eligible for personal help under German social law, including support described by Berlin under SGB XII section 67. This page only summarizes the route; applications and case-specific questions should be handled with qualified advice.

Helping responsibly

Helpful action should begin with respect and consent. If direct contact feels safe and appropriate, ask what the person wants before calling services or deciding what they need. Concrete offers such as water, food, a warm drink, looking up a nearby support point, or calling a service can be more useful than general advice.
